Background

Wanting to gain a larger breadth of the product cycle outside of research and design, I challenged myself with learning front end development skills. Once I learned the basics, my first mission was simple: fully develop my own website.

Project Type & Involvement

Develop and improve a fully functional website.

Timeline

Jun 2022 - June 2022

Platforms

iOS, Android & Web

DEVELOPMENT:

After going through Google’s Design Sprint methodology (read more about that below), I was able to design and implement a fully working website. These are some of the key features that became foundational to the site.

Responsive Navigation:

For this component, I worked to change the layout of my navigation depending on what form of technology the user has (phone, tablet, desktop). I did so using media queries, which trigger different properties depending on the screen size. I chose the layout of each navigation based on what was common for other sites. This was to create a sense of familiarity in the website for the users, as to make the learning curve of the site smaller for a first time user.

Above you can watch me tab through the intro / work page showing both the skip to content as well as the accessible cards.

User Applications

Accessibility

Accessibility is something that is incredibly important to me. Everyone should be able to use the products we make and it is unethical for us as designers to disclude communities from our work. In this project, I was determined to make sure my website was keyboard accessible, not just mouse accessible. To show this, I designed some bootstrap labels,scroll effects,or even animations.

Interaction:

I chose to add interactive elements to each page to engage users as they browse the site. On the intro/job page, there is an animated background effect. Then there's the dislocation animation on the blog page.
Next, there is a contact page parallel to them, which will jump out after clicking.Besides that, you’ll found pages include many color change upon hover for the button.These features helped me to engage my audience and flex my developer muscles.

DESIGN PROCESS:

Initiating the project, I began with design. To add a real-world layer to this task, I decided to follow the Google Design Thinking Process and treated this project as a design sprint. Below you can see the diamond-shaped phases of Google’s sprint process.

Phase 1 - Understand:

Having set up the constraint of “exploring a passion”, I knew Minimalist(style) was what I wanted to focus on. But how could I explain "style" to users in a way that has yet to be done? To better understand my passions, and how to make “through web development” impactful, I created some “How might I” questions as a take on the “How might we” exercise that Google classifies under the “understand” phase.


After my HMI (how might I) questions, being that I was in a group of one and democratic voting wouldn’t help chose the best, I decided to pull out some high-level topics each question posed and pull together a final HMI based on the most relevant topics. This then led me to my final HMI question I wanted to solve:
”How might we increase awareness of the full product offerings?”

Phase 2 - Define

Now that I had a better understanding of what I was going to accomplish, I needed to define how I was going to create a solution. It was time to set guiding principles and goals to define the attitude of my design sprint. I began phase two with the “Design Principles” exercise; this exercise establishes the design values of the project as a reference for future design decisions.

I spent 10 minutes thinking of different principles that I felt would guide this project nicely. From there, I grouped the stickies into general groups to find my guiding principles. The 3 categories I fell upon were:

1.intuiteive:the user makes intentional choices based on the humansistic context of the design
2.simple:consistent mimimalistic features create a clean interface
3.usable:—intuitively usable to the degree the user is able to interact with it effectively by applying knowledge unconsciously.

Phase 3 - Sketch:

After phase 2, I decided to hone in on which contents I wanted to feature. Coming from a personal love, I decided on Manga topic and as my group of choice. In phase 3, I brainstormed basic sketches to get ideas flowing for how the design may solve the problem. Specifically in this phase, I only focused on the navigation layout and the home screen, just to create a baseline of design for the project.

Phase 4 - Decide:

It is now time to finalize the design direction before prototyping. Before I went into the design direction, I wanted to create an information architecture to finalize the content of the site and not miss out on important criteria. I created a site map for myself categorizing the important navigation structure, content hierarchy, and more.

——Being that I was in a team of one, I created my first set of wireframes and presented them to my peers to gain initial feedback of simplicity and intuitiveness, two of my guiding design principles. In this case, I used the “Present Solution Sketches” exercise to hear initial feedback for testing. In this method, I presented my wireframes for about 3 minutes then had an open conversation afterwards to gain insight.

TAKEAWAYS:

1. Constraints can be a blessing

When working with the design, I needed to keep in mind what I was capable of completing from a development standpoint. The constraints of my coding abilities limited my design practice. But, through exploring my development capabilities, I was able to add features that I hadn’t previously considered. Because of this, these constraints acted as bumpers to keep me focused on my design principle of Simplicity.

2. A world of one

While it was nice having autonomy over this project, it made me realize how much I value working in team settings. Many times I leaned on my peers to ask “does this look okay?” or “what do you think of this feature?” Often times, they just gave their own judgment and moved on, not taking into consideration the goals and principles of the project, being that they weren’t personally invested in the project.
